A 62-year-old male presents to the ED with substernal chest pressure for approximately 2 hours, onset at rest. He describes the pain as heavy pressure radiating to the left arm and jaw, associated with shortness of breath, nausea, and diaphoresis. Pain is constant since onset, not relieved by rest. Past medical history significant for hypertension and hyperlipidemia. Family history notable for father with myocardial infarction at age 55. Twenty-pack-year smoking history. Home medications include lisinopril 20mg daily, atorvastatin 40mg daily, and aspirin 81mg daily. No known drug allergies. Vitals: BP 162/98, HR 94, RR 20, SpO2 96% on room air, T 98.4°F. Exam: anxious and diaphoretic, regular rate and rhythm, no murmurs rubs or gallops, lungs clear to auscultation bilaterally, no lower extremity edema. 12-lead ECG shows ST elevation in leads V2 through V5 with reciprocal ST depression in leads II, III, and aVF. Initial troponin I elevated at 0.82 ng/mL. CBC within normal limits. BMP notable for glucose 142, Cr 1.0. PT/INR normal. Assessment: acute anterior STEMI. Plan: cath lab activated, aspirin 325mg chewed and swallowed, ticagrelor 180mg loading dose, heparin bolus 60 units/kg, morphine 4mg IV for pain, supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula at 2L. Cardiology en route for emergent PCI. Admit to CCU post-procedure.
